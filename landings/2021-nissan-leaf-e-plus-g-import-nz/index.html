Model and compliance notes

Here’s how the 2021 Nissan Leaf E+ G typically lines up when you’re comparing options to import.

Year range
The E+ G with the 62 kWh battery is available from around 2019 onwards, with 2021 cars benefiting from newer battery age and updated safety tech. Later years can help with future resale and may show less battery wear, but they usually cost more at auction.
Engine options
As a full EV, the Leaf E+ G uses a 62 kWh lithium-ion battery and 160 kW motor only, with no petrol backup. Earlier or lower trims often have a 40 kWh pack, which is cheaper but offers less range, so think about your daily distance and charging options.
Key trim differences
The E+ G trim typically adds the larger 62 kWh battery, 160 kW motor, 17-inch alloys and more safety and comfort features compared with base models. Some cars will also have extras like ProPILOT, heated seats and upgraded audio, which we can confirm from the auction sheet.
NZ compliance notes
2021 Leafs typically meet NZ entry standards if documentation is clean and no modifications are noted. As an EV there’s no Clean Car fee, and you may qualify for lower running costs, but normal entry certification checks still apply.
Auction availability
Leaf E+ G models appear regularly at major Japanese auctions, but low-kilometre, high-SOH examples attract strong bidding. If you’re flexible on colour, mileage or options, we can usually find a suitable car more quickly.

Home charging

Charging a 2021 Leaf E+ G in NZ

Most owners charge at home using a dedicated wall unit or a standard socket, topping up the 62 kWh battery overnight. This lets you leave each morning with a full charge and keeps running costs low, especially if you use off-peak power rates.

Public charging

On the road, the Leaf E+ G uses CHAdeMO fast charging, which is available across much of NZ at supermarkets, shopping centres and highway stops. You can also use slower AC chargers in many public carparks while you shop or work.

  • • CHAdeMO is well established in NZ but newer networks may focus more on CCS, so check coverage on your regular routes.
  • • Fast charging too often can add wear to the battery over time, so it’s best kept for trips rather than daily use.
  • • Charging speeds can slow down as the battery fills or in hot conditions, so allow some buffer time on long journeys.

What real-world range can I expect from a 2021 Leaf E+ G in NZ?

The WLTP figure is up to 385 km, but in NZ conditions most drivers see less, especially at motorway speeds or in winter. We use SOH, your driving pattern and local terrain to give a realistic range estimate before you buy.

How much does it cost to import a 2021 Nissan Leaf E+ G?

Most buyers land these between about $32,000 and $42,000, depending on mileage, SOH, grade and options. The total includes the auction price, our fee, shipping, insurance, NZ entry certification, port charges and on-road costs.

Will a 2021 Leaf E+ G usually meet NZ compliance standards?

Based on auction documentation, this model usually meets NZ entry standards if documentation is clean and no modifications are noted. Final approval depends on physical inspection at entry certification, which we can’t guarantee in advance.

How long does it take from winning an auction to driving the car in NZ?

From a successful bid, allow roughly 6–10 weeks for shipping, port processing, NZ entry certification and registration. Timing varies with shipping schedules, port congestion and how quickly compliance inspections can be completed.

How do auction grades relate to the condition of a Leaf E+ G?

Auction grades give a snapshot of exterior, interior and overall condition, but the comments and photos matter just as much. We translate key notes, highlight any repairs or damage and give you our view so you’re not buying blind.
