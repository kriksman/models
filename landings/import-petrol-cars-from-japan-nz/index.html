Why Kiwis buy this import

Landed-cost clarity

Model and compliance notes

Most buyers narrow their search to a few petrol models before we start bidding.

Year range
Later years usually mean more safety tech and lower mileage but higher auction prices. We also check that the build year meets current NZ entry rules, including ESC requirements for light vehicles.
Powertrain or battery options
Petrol imports can range from small-displacement engines aimed at economy through to larger units with more power. We explain how each option affects fuel use, road tax and day-to-day running costs.
Key trim differences
Higher trims may add features like better seats, climate control, parking sensors or upgraded audio. We point out which features matter in NZ and which are mainly cosmetic.
NZ compliance notes
We look for ESC, airbag and structural notes on the auction sheet and check that the car typically meets NZ entry standards if documentation is clean and no modifications are noted.
Auction availability
Common models appear daily and give more choice on colour, mileage and grade. Rarer models may take longer to find and can attract stronger bidding at auction.

We look closely at auction notes for oil leaks, timing belt or chain comments, transmission behaviour and any mention of modifications. Confirm whether the engine uses a timing belt or chain and when it was last serviced, if recorded.
Check auction notes for oil leaks, engine noise or smoke on start-up.
Review transmission comments for slipping, harsh shifts or warning lights.
Look for any mention of aftermarket modifications to intake, exhaust or suspension.
Check drivetrain layout (FWD, RWD, AWD) matches how and where you plan to drive.

Alternatives

If a petrol import doesn’t quite line up with your needs, there are other paths to consider.

Hybrid import from Japan

Better if: You want lower fuel use and do a lot of city driving.

Trade-off: Hybrid systems can be more complex and may have higher repair costs if something fails out of warranty.

NZ-new petrol car

Better if: You prefer local service history and being able to see the car in person before buying.

Trade-off: You’ll usually pay more for similar age and mileage compared with a Japanese import.

Used diesel SUV or ute

Better if: You tow regularly or cover high open-road mileage.

Trade-off: Diesel road user charges and potential emissions-related maintenance need to be factored into running costs.

FAQ

Questions before importing

What types of petrol cars can I import from Japan?

You can import a wide range of petrol vehicles, from small hatchbacks and sedans through to SUVs, people movers and some utes. We’ll help you narrow it down based on budget, size, features and how you plan to use the car.

How much does it cost to import a petrol car from Japan?

Total cost depends on the auction price, shipping, marine insurance, NZ port charges, entry certification, compliance work and our service fee. We provide a landed cost estimate before bidding so you can see how different auction prices affect your budget.

Will my imported petrol car meet NZ compliance standards?

We target cars that, based on auction documentation, usually meet NZ entry standards if documentation is clean and no modifications are noted. Final approval is made at NZ entry certification once the car is physically inspected.

How long does it take to get a petrol car from Japan to NZ?

Once we win an auction, shipping to NZ typically takes 2–4 weeks depending on the sailing schedule and port. Allow extra time for export processing in Japan, NZ entry certification and any compliance work needed.

What condition are Japanese petrol imports usually in?

Condition varies by age, mileage and use. Auction grades and comments give a good indication of exterior, interior and mechanical state. We talk you through the sheet, highlight any red flags and only recommend bidding on cars where the condition matches your expectations and budget.
